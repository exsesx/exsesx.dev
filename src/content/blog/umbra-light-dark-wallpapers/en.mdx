I have used Dark Mode for so long that switching my Mac back to Light Mode felt
like a bigger change than it should have. The apps looked fine, but the desktop
kept bothering me.

The wallpaper does more for the feel of macOS than I usually notice. I wanted one image
for Light Mode and another for Dark Mode, with the desktop following the system
automatically. I did not need the image to change with the sun or on a schedule.
I only wanted a pair.

## The pair I wanted

Raycast has a lovely
<SourceLink href="https://www.raycast.com/wallpapers">wallpaper collection</SourceLink>.
I kept coming back to two images from it:
<SourceLink href="https://misc-assets.raycast.com/wallpapers/mono_light_distortion_1.heic">`mono_light_distortion_1`</SourceLink>
and
<SourceLink href="https://misc-assets.raycast.com/wallpapers/mono_dark_distortion_1.heic">`mono_dark_distortion_1`</SourceLink>.
They belong together, and each has the right contrast for its appearance. The
light one does not look washed out. The dark one does not turn the desktop into
a bright rectangle behind every window.

## The options that almost worked

<SourceLink href="https://equinoxmac.com/">Equinox</SourceLink> can make an
appearance-based macOS wallpaper from two images. It is free, open source, and
also handles solar and time-based wallpapers. That makes sense if I want to
create and export a proper dynamic wallpaper. For this job, it felt like too
much machinery.

I was already using the
<SourceLink href="https://www.raycast.com/koinzhang/raycast-wallpaper">Raycast Wallpaper extension</SourceLink>.
It has exactly two commands: `Set Raycast Wallpaper` and
`Auto Switch Raycast Wallpaper`. Both expose a `Respect System Appearance`
setting, so saying that the extension ignores Light and Dark Mode would be
wrong.

The difference is what that setting does. The extension classifies Raycast's
catalogue as light or dark. It can show only wallpapers for the current
appearance, or narrow the pool before choosing one at random. The current
<SourceLink href="https://github.com/raycast/extensions/blob/main/extensions/raycast-wallpaper/package.json">manifest</SourceLink>
and
<SourceLink href="https://github.com/raycast/extensions/blob/main/extensions/raycast-wallpaper/src/auto-switch-raycast-wallpaper.ts">auto-switch implementation</SourceLink>
do not provide a persistent slot for one chosen Light wallpaper and another for
Dark Mode.

I could probably build a workaround around those commands. Then I would be
maintaining a workaround for a preference that should take two clicks.

## Umbra is just right

<SourceLink href="https://replay.software/umbra">Umbra</SourceLink> matches the
way I was already thinking about the problem: one place for the Light wallpaper
and one for the Dark wallpaper. I drop in the two images, and Umbra keeps them in
sync with the macOS appearance. It can also toggle Dark Mode from the menu bar.

That direct setup is why I kept it. Umbra has more options, including
per-display wallpapers and better handling for HEIC files, but I do not have to
turn my simple preference into a wallpaper project. Replay offers it as
"pay what you like," which also makes it easy to try.

## A small Replay aside

Umbra was not my first app from Replay. I also use
<SourceLink href="https://replay.software/echo">Echo</SourceLink>, its SSH and
Mosh client for iPhone and iPad. I reach for it less now that I can
<SourceLink href="https://openai.com/index/work-with-codex-from-anywhere/">follow Codex work from the ChatGPT mobile app</SourceLink>
and Claude has
<SourceLink href="https://www.anthropic.com/news/finance-agents">Dispatch</SourceLink>,
but Echo is still a neat project. It takes a job that is easy to get wrong on a
phone and makes it feel native.

Umbra feels similarly focused. It does not ask me to change how I manage
appearance in macOS. It handles the missing wallpaper pair and stays out of the
way.

## Why this small thing matters to me

I spend hours looking at macOS, so this minor tweak is always in view. The
desktop sits behind every window. It affects the contrast around the edges and
is often the first thing I see when I switch tasks.

That is why I like small utilities that let me make the OS mine without adding a
new ritual. My Mac now has those two Raycast images in Umbra. Light Mode finally
feels like a complete theme instead of a temporary setting I will switch off in
ten minutes.
